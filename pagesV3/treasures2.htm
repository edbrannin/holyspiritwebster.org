Holy Spirit Parish Treasures (pg 2)

February 24, 2008
Let us return to the Main Entrance as we explore the treasures of our church. One first encounters the Baptismal area which, as I explained previously, reminds us that we are here because of our own baptism which made us members of God‘s family, the Church. Behind the Baptismal Font are icons of the twelve great feasts of the church year recalling the great events of Salvation History from Mary‘s birth through the life of Christ up to Mary‘s dormition. Atop the Ambry is a special shrine which reflects the season we are in. Bright banners celebrate the new life of Baptism and the Easter Candle announces our salvation through our participation in the Death and Resurrection of Christ. The candle is moved to be near the casket at a Funeral and during Easter time is enshrined next to the pulpit. A new candle is lit at the Easter Vigil to announce the Resurrection.

You are asking now – what is the .Ambry.? That is the box on the wall behind the font where the Holy Oils, blessed by the Bishop during Holy week, are kept. Do you know what the three oils are called? Tune in next week! I close by mentioning the artistic image of the Holy Trinity on the back of the Font which reminds us that we are baptized in the name of the Father, Son and Holy Spirit.

March 2, 2008
Did you know the names of the three Holy Oils? They are: Oil of Catechumens used in Baptism, Oil of the Sick used in the sacrament of Anointing of the Sick, and Sacred Chrism, the fragrant oil used in consecrating persons and things such as altars and churches. It is used to show that we are to carry on the work of the “anointed one” (Christ) as we are baptized, confirmed or receive Holy Orders. Have you ever attended the Chrism Mass at the cathedral now celebrated on Tuesday of Holy Week? Every one should go at least once. After the Bishop blesses the oils they are distributed to the parishes reminding us that the Bishop is “super pastor” who assigns Priest or Pastoral Ministers to shepherd each parish.

Since there are no Baptisms during Lent, we may remove the water early again this year to allow in the Font the Life of Christ display donated by Debbie Pellegrino. Watch for it.

March 9, 2008
It is taking us a long time just to get into the church. Complementing the baptismal font where we become members of the church and are first given the life of grace are the confessionals along the west wall of the church. Here we can be restored to the church if serious sin cuts us off, regain or strengthen our life of grace as needed and receive guidance on how to advance in our spiritual life. It is here that our Loving Father (God) waits for his .prodigal sons and daughters.. Between the confessionals we find the huge copy of Juan Diego’s tilma containing Our Lady’s picture. Our Lady of Guadalupe is Patroness of all the Americas and of Right to Life efforts. The beautiful woodwork was custom made by our own adult and youth parishioners. She is truly our mother who leads us to her son, Jesus. To your left as you enter, is an alcove we call the .shrine area. which originally contained only the holy family and was the only place religious devotional art was found in the church or chapel. Today, numerous images of saints are there as well as displays of the days’ saints surrounding the Word of God enshrined in the center with a candle burning. Do you know what saints are represented there?

 

 

 

 

 

 

March 16, 2008
It seems appropriate as we start Holy Week to focus on the Stations of the Cross in the church. While officially it is only necessary to have a wooden cross to mark the 14 or 15 .stations., usually the event of each stop on the Way of the Cross is depicted in some manner. In some old churches, they are huge. Ours are a modest size, simple depiction the events leading to Jesus’ death and burial. Some have a .resurrection. station. We do not – unless you use the Easter Candle for this purpose. Can you name the 14 events – have you and your children made the Way of the Cross at least once during lent? Complementing the Stations are two donations from parishioners – a .Pieta. Mary holding her dead son (blanket made by parishioner) and a large painting of the Descent from the Cross which may have been in St. Bernard’s Seminary. This is moved to the alter area during Holy week and so is there today.

March 30, 2008
Visitors often comment .why is the church off centered?. New parishioners may wonder the same thing. The church was built during a brief era when it was thought that the church building of the future would be a multi-purpose building. The worship center would contain a small permanent daily chapel with tabernacle, pews, etc. The bulk of the building would be used for ALL KINDS of activities including Sunday Mass. This proved to be unworkable and undesirable and all such churches in our Diocese have become permanent churches. However, we still retain the daily chapel for Mass and Adoration as separate from the main church, although there is no wall. Some churches now have .gathering areas. that can expand the size of the church when needed.
Originally the church was bare of any ornamentation or devotional art. Only the essentials for Mass were present. When the Parish Center was built, Father Bush covered the walls and added the wood strips on the sanctuary wall. More on church structure to follow.

April 13, 2008
The last article two weeks ago explained the origins of the church building. This article continues in that vein. Traditionally one faced east to pray toward the rising sun, while we actually face south in our building, the edifice itself faces east, rising higher and higher to the east wall and the decorative wood work on the south wall flows in a rising pattern toward the east. We face east as we enter the church as well. The high windows which let the rising and setting suns into the building allow the sun to sometimes provide unusual and inspiring patterns within the church.
On the four walls are found small wooden crosses . one on each wall . which signify that the church as been consecrated . now set apart only for spiritual events, on weekends candles of the seasons liturgical color are placed on these crosses. Currently the carpeting shows the damage from our former roof problems. We await a new look. in June after the new carpeting is installed. Beauty, art and ambiance all enhance our worship experience.

April 20, 2008
What we now refer to as the chapel. was originally the permanent worship area but still did not have much decoration. The tabernacle and pews made it different from the larger, higher area. Retaining the chapel altar even after the multi-purpose area became permanent church allows daily mass to be offered in a more intimate, less formal setting. It also provides a suitable place for adoration. One of the first things I did after becoming pastor in 2001 was to give the parish the angels which hold two of the four adoration candles. I think that they help set the area aside for adoration.
The risen Christ on the back wall rising above the monstrance reminds us that Jesus, now risen and glorified, is present in our midst Body and Blood, Soul and Divinity. During Lent the Risen Jesus (like the Alleluia) is removed so that we better experience and long for his return at Easter. By the pillar is the daily shrine. where the saint of the day or feast or season is featured. A statue of St. Jude donated by Phyllis Germano and a statue of St. Ann restored by Ann Sakmyster also are in the chapel. The sanctuary lamp is fairly new in a more traditional style than the plain, black original one was. We thank Jane Galvin for that. Have you noticed the small magnet. saints on the pillars? Since the altar was built too close to the step, some parishioners are building an extension to be installed with the new carpeting. This would allow the priest to stand in front of the Altar for occasions such as Mass in the extraordinary form.
